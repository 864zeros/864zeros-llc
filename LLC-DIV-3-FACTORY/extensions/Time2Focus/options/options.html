Why Time2Focus Works

ADHD brains struggle with time blindness and working memory. Traditional timers don't help because they disappear — and so does your focus. Time2Focus stays visible in your side panel, constantly reminding you what you're supposed to be doing.

Always Visible

The side panel stays open while you browse. Your focus topic and countdown are always in peripheral vision.

Time Boxing

Preset intervals (5, 10, 25, 50 min) make starting effortless. No decision fatigue about "how long should I work?"

Focus Anchor

Write what you're working on. When you get distracted (not if), glance right and remember your intention.

Gentle Alerts

Soft sounds and a breathing color pulse — not jarring alarms. Designed for sensory-sensitive brains.

The Science Behind It

Time2Focus is built on research about ADHD, time perception, and task management. Here's what the science says:

Time Blindness

ADHD and Time Perception

Research shows that people with ADHD have impaired time perception, often underestimating how much time has passed. External time cues (like a visible countdown) help compensate for this deficit.

Barkley, R. A. (1997). "ADHD and the Nature of Self-Control." Guilford Press.

Pomodoro Technique

Time Boxing for Focus

The Pomodoro Technique (working in timed intervals) has been shown to improve focus and reduce procrastination. The key is making the time constraint visible and external, not relying on internal time sense.

Cirillo, F. (2006). "The Pomodoro Technique." FC Garage.

Working Memory

External Memory Aids

ADHD affects working memory — the ability to hold information in mind while doing a task. Writing down your current focus acts as an "external working memory," reducing cognitive load.

Klingberg, T. (2009). "The Overflowing Brain." Oxford University Press.

Implementation Intentions

Naming Your Focus

Research on "implementation intentions" shows that explicitly stating what you'll do ("I am focused on X") dramatically increases follow-through, especially for people who struggle with executive function.

Gollwitzer, P. M. (1999). "Implementation Intentions." American Psychologist.

How to Use

  1. Open the side panel — Click the Time2Focus icon in your toolbar
  2. Name your focus — Type what you're working on in the input field
  3. Pick a time — Tap 5, 10, 25, or 50 minutes
  4. Work — The timer and your focus topic stay visible
  5. Get alerted — Gentle sound and color pulse when time's up
  6. Repeat — Start another session or take a break
